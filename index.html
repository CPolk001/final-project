Ins, Outs, Ups and Downs about all things Percussion


Percussion: the backbeat of music. What exactly is percussion? There are many instruments that fit under
the percussion umbrella, but all of them can be categorized into four main types:

Pitched

Pitched Percussion utilizes tuning notes, otherwise referred to as 'pitches', to alter what sound the instrument makes. Typical pitched percussion includes mallet instruments such as marimba and xylophone, tunable drums such as timpani and roto toms, and other auxillary instruments like triangles and woodblocks.

Unpitched

Unpitched Percussion, although some are tunable to a pitch, includes typical drums and auxillary percussion. Many of the common auxillary percussion instruments are often used for sound effects, such as castanets, guiros, rainsticks, and tambourines.

Body

Body percussion utilizes the body to create sound. The most common sounds are claps, slaps, and stomps. There are often many pieces of music for percussion that require the player to create the sound with their body.

Wind

The last category, though sometimes argued as anything but percussion, is wind. These instruments require the use of wind to create a sound, such as the slide whistle, kazoo, and air horn. Often played by percussion, these also accompany pieces to create specific auditory sound effects required in the ensemble.
